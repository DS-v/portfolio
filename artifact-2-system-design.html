Portfolio Artifact 02 Platform & Systems Design

Designing an end-to-end
cross-border procurement
system as a decision-system

Most platforms in this market don't fail because a feature is missing. They fail because a decision is made too early, too late, or without clear ownership. This artifact documents how I structured decisions, risk, and ownership across a messy, ops-heavy market — not a feature walkthrough, but the reasoning underneath one.

L3 System Design 9 Decision Layers Pre-PO / Post-PO Human-in-the-Loop
How to read this

Quick skim (5 min): Executive summary, the L3 map, and the decision-layer headings.

Deeper read (10–15 min): Read the decision layers themselves and the human-in-the-loop boundaries.

It isn't meant to be read linearly — it's built to be scanned and jumped into.

01

The thesis: turn operational expertise into platform leverage

The design goal was never to automate the human element away. It was to transform hard-won operational judgment into something a platform can scale — and to be deliberate about where that judgment must stay human.

Cross-border trade is defined by three cold-start bottlenecks. Every decision layer in the system exists to strengthen one of them. If a layer didn't serve a pillar, it didn't earn a place in the design.

02

The L3 map: two phases, nine decision layers

An L3 design is about responsibilities, decision boundaries, and stateful objects — not boxes-and-arrows for their own sake. The system splits into two phases with fundamentally different risk profiles.

Pre-PO — Decision Formation

Decisions are exploratory and reversible. The cost of a wrong call is a redo, not a loss. Automation can lean forward here.

Post-PO — Execution Under Commitment

Decisions are binding and risk-bearing. Legal, financial, and reputational exposure is live. Automation must defer to humans at every irreversible edge.

Each layer answers exactly one question and produces one concrete system object. That discipline is what keeps ownership legible: if you can name the object a layer emits, you can name who is accountable for it.

03

Two infrastructures that run across everything

Some capabilities don't belong to a phase. They sit underneath all of them. Getting their boundaries right matters more than getting their features right.

Communication Fabric

Centralizes and archives commercial context on-platform — without pulling WhatsApp or email channels into the system. Automated nudges and email alerts handle predictable state transitions; manual intervention stays open for nuance.

The boundary that matters

It monitors state but never executes a commercial commitment. That single rule is what keeps a communication layer from quietly becoming a transaction layer it was never designed to be trusted with.

Risk Flagging & Delay Prediction

Phase-independent oversight: identifying systemic risk patterns, modelling probabilistic logistics outcomes, and codifying proactive interventions for high-risk exceptions.

The design philosophy

Risk is a perpetual variable — actively managed from intent through final settlement, not a check you run once. Outputs: a live risk-flag stream, fulfilment timelines, delay forecasts, escalation routing.

04

Pre-PO — where decisions are still reversible

Reversible Exploratory decisions. The cost of being wrong is a redo. Automation can lean forward.
Layer 1

Intent Formation & Sourceability

What exactly are we trying to source, in a form suppliers can realistically quote?
Buyers submit RFQs with whatever they have — text, documents, drawings. The system parses into structured line items and the buyer confirms intent. AI normalises specs and units, flags ambiguity and conflicts, and raises clarifications when confidence is low. Ops resolves exceptions before any supplier sees the RFQ. Ambiguity collapses once, upstream, instead of every supplier interpreting it independently.
Output → Canonical RFQ
Layer 2

Supplier Discovery & Liquidity

Who can credibly supply this, given the actual constraints?
Retrieval and rules match canonical RFQs against known supplier capabilities. The goal isn't perfect ranking — it's avoiding obvious mismatches and surfacing credible options. When coverage or confidence is weak, fallback sourcing triggers. Discovery can be automated; validation cannot — Ops verifies legitimacy before a supplier is pulled in. Liquidity here is engineered, not assumed.
Output → Supplier–item match set
Layer 3

Commercial Signal Collection

What are suppliers actually willing to commit to?
Line items are batched per supplier and sent in a form that minimises rework. Suppliers respond however they normally do; the system structures prices, lead times, and terms, and suppliers confirm what was extracted.
Output → Structured quote set
Layer 4

Risk Filtering & Choice Support

Which offers are acceptable, and which of those should we choose?
Four moves, deliberately sequenced and separated:
  • Technical approval — binary. Quotes pass or they don't, before price ever enters the picture.
  • Comparison — read-only. Surfaces tradeoffs across price, lead time, terms, and supplier reliability through ratings, but never mutates supplier state and never decides for the buyer.
  • Negotiation — the one place supplier state changes, intentionally walled off from comparison.
  • Landed cost — computed from rules and learned signals; Ops validates because errors here propagate downstream.
Output → Final quote set + Landed cost view
Layer 5

Commitment Boundary

Are we ready to commit commercially?
When buyers choose suppliers and terms, the system guides them through checkout and the formal placement of a Purchase Order, creating an Order Intent. This is the last reversible state in the entire system. Everything after it carries legal, financial, or reputational risk — which is exactly why it gets its own layer rather than being folded into the step before or after.
Output → Order Intent
The line everything pivots on

Order Intent is the last reversible state. The architecture is organized around protecting that line — not around any single feature.

05

Post-PO — where every decision is binding

Binding Legal, financial, reputational exposure is live. Automation defers to humans at every irreversible edge.
Layer 6

Execution Planning

How should this order be executed?
The platform converts a commercial commitment into an executable plan: HSN classification, hazardous-goods identification, QC/QAP requirement determination, supplier payment planning, logistics planning. Ops validates recommendations before approval.
Output → Execution Plan
Layer 7

Supplier Commitment & Financial Orchestration

Are execution participants aligned on the plan?
Supplier-specific POs are generated, but Ops controls final release. The platform orchestrates supplier invoices, buyer invoices, and payment milestones — while fund movement itself happens outside the platform.
Output → Seller PO · Payment state
Layer 8

Shipment Readiness

Is this shipment ready to move?
The strongest trust boundary in the platform — a sequence of gates, each Ops-validated:
  • Seller execution — manufacture/procure, prepare docs, ready items for inspection and dispatch.
  • QC validation — AI-assisted visual inspection for quantity, visible defects, labelling; third parties loop in for advanced cases.
  • Packing validation — checklist-driven review detecting packing non-compliance.
  • Document validation — invoice, packing list, e-way bill, dispatch and compliance docs checked for inconsistencies and gaps.
  • Buyer dispatch approval — the human authorization that releases the shipment.
Output → Dispatch Approval
Layer 9

Logistics Orchestration & Visibility

How should cargo move, and how do stakeholders stay informed?
Domestic consolidation, freight-forwarding coordination, customs coordination, and cargo tracking with location-based shipment visibility — keeping every stakeholder current as the shipment moves.
Output → Shipment State
06

Where automation stops — on purpose

There are places where automation simply isn't worth the risk. These aren't gaps in the build — they're deliberate boundaries. Failures here don't degrade UX; they destroy trust, and trust in this market doesn't come back cheaply.

07

The tradeoffs I chose — and the ones I rejected

Design tradeoffs

  • Automation stops where failure becomes irreversible. The threshold is a feature, not a limitation.
  • Ops is treated as leverage, not as a failure mode. Human judgment is designed-in, not apologized for.
  • Liquidity is qualified before it is scaled. Credibility precedes volume.
Alternative considered — Why not auto-award to the lowest price?

Because the cost of failure is asymmetric. A bad cheap award can cost far more than the spread it saved. The system optimizes for risk-adjusted outcomes, not price minima.

Alternative considered — Why not fully self-serve PO issuance?

Because early trust failures cost more than the ops savings are worth. In a market built on credibility, a self-serve mistake doesn't just cost one order — it costs the relationship.

A failure we chose to accept

We accepted slower turnaround for edge-case, highly ambiguous RFQs. The alternative was faster throughput at the cost of systemic errors and supplier distrust. We chose correctness and trust over speed at the edges — deliberately, with eyes open.

08

What we measured — and what we refused to

We avoided vanity metrics and tracked only what reflected decision quality, supplier effort, and operational leverage.

RFQ → Valid Quote Rate
Did structured intent produce quotable, committable supplier responses?
Time to First Confident Quote
How quickly does a buyer reach a quote they can trust — and are suppliers prioritizing platform RFQs?
Qualified Quotes per RFQ
Technically approved, comparable quotes per RFQ — not raw quote count.
Quote → PO Conversion
Did the choice-support layer lead to commitments that held?
What this artifact is really demonstrating

Not a feature set — a decision-system. The skill on display is knowing where a decision belongs, who owns it, and when software should stop and defer to a human.
