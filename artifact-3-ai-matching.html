AI Matching: what everyone expects vs. what actually works

Designing supplier matching for high-risk B2B procurement — where a wrong match doesn't just hurt UX, it burns a supplier relationship you can't get back.

  • Domain · Cross-border industrial procurement
  • Scale · 1–3k suppliers, 10k+ product types
  • Read · ~12 min
Executive summary

How AI supplier matching was designed, constrained, and evaluated inside an ops-heavy B2B procurement system — building on the failure modes in Artifact 1 and the procurement system in Artifact 2. Here a wrong match isn't a UX problem: it raises supplier effort, causes regret and ghosting, and lowers willingness to quote on the next RFQ.

Three coupled problems run through it — data formation, matching, and decision quality. The design move was the same across all three: cast wide in retrieval, narrow hard through reasoning and confidence floors, and refuse to automate the irreversible calls.

Core takeaway: most matching failures came not from weak models, but from under-designed data, evaluation, and risk boundaries.
01

The matching hypothesis everyone starts with

The standard AI-marketplace playbook is clean, intuitive, and — in this environment — quietly wrong in its assumptions.

Embed supplier catalogs. Embed RFQ line items into the same space. Let similarity search surface relevant suppliers. Have an LLM score and rank. Add business rules as guardrails. It's a reasonable hypothesis: manual discovery doesn't scale, and matching looks like the bottleneck.

What this underestimates isn't modeling complexity. It's systemic dependency — messy inputs, supplier incentives, and the asymmetric cost of being wrong. The model was never the hard part. Everything feeding the model was.

02

Why catalogs are messy — and why that's the real problem

Before matching quality can improve, the system needs a truthful representation of what a supplier can actually provide. Forming that proved harder than the matching itself.

Supplier catalogs are PDFs, Excel files, scanned brochures, and WhatsApp images — marketing-oriented, not operationally complete, highlighting strengths rather than boundaries. The dominant failure mode is structural, not syntactic: naive parsing produces records that are clean but semantically empty.

The structural traps that defeat naive extraction

Across real catalogs — conveyor belting, gearboxes, insulating mats, plywood — the failures cluster into three families. None is a chunking problem; each is a way the document's structure carries meaning that flat text extraction destroys before any model sees it.

Two further traps recur everywhere. Variants hide in ranges and footnotes — a gearbox rated "5:1 to 490:1" needs parsing into {min, max} and a numeric check, not similarity (more in §3). And brand wears four different hats — the same string can be the supplier's own brand, a resold brand, a customer logo (the Tata, Adani, or KOC logos that appear in these very catalogs), or a product grade. Inheriting it naively can attach a customer's logo to a product as if it were for sale.

The response: read with vision, carry context forward

Catalogs are ingested through a single stateful vision-LLM sweep over page images, and each property answers one family of traps. Vision reads 2-D tables as tables — rebuilding nested headers, merged cells, transposed layouts — and reads the diagrams and silhouettes where meaning lives outside the text (families 1–2). Statefulness carries brand, certifications, standards, and units forward from cover and section heads, inheriting them onto items pages later (family 3). And a fact is only ever inherited from where it was stated, never guessed — closing the inference trap by refusing to fill a field the catalog didn't support.

The output is one self-contained record per (product line × brand), so a leaf never reads as "CDH8409, 9mm" with no idea what it is, what it's certified to, or who makes it. Where the source itself is wrong — a repeated "ST1000" header — that's surfaced for review rather than silently propagated.

Validated, not assumed ~50–55 representative catalogs were manually analyzed for product coverage, spec fields, and manufacturer–product links. This ingestion work moved the needle on valid-quote rates more than any downstream ranking tweak.
03

Why embeddings weren't enough

Embeddings are excellent at paraphrase and terrible at exactly the tokens this domain depends on. That mismatch quietly costs recall where it hurts most.

The expectation

One semantic space. RFQ and catalog meet by cosine similarity. The model "understands" the match.

The reality

LMV52.200B1 and "Siemens combustion manager" aren't close in vector space. Neither are 70 m³/hr and "70 cubic mtrs/hr." Neither is a 250:1 ratio against the text "5:1 to 490:1."

Two failures, distinct in kind. Vocabulary mismatch: "Siemens" vs "Siemens India," "PCHENIX" (a real typo) vs "Phoenix Contact," "SS304" vs "Stainless Steel 304." And structured-attribute matching: part numbers, unit-bearing specs, numeric ranges — none of which similarity handles reliably. Leaning on the embedding to absorb these silently under-recalls on the highest-value, most specific terms in the request.

The response: retrieve broadly, reason narrowly

Nothing hard-filters. Hybrid retrieval — dense embeddings for genuine paraphrase, BM25 for the lexical tokens embeddings fumble, and a dedicated part-number lane — does recall. Category and brand are ranking biases, never filters, so an inconsistent taxonomy or a mis-resolved brand costs a candidate a few rank positions instead of silently excluding it. Then an LLM makes the actual decision.

One discipline holds this together: normalization is symmetric. "Siemens" matches "Siemens India" only because the same canonicalizer — brand aliases, unit conversion, taxonomy — runs on both the catalog and the RFQ. Normalize one side only, and you reintroduce every mismatch at the point of comparison.
04

Precision vs recall is a supplier-economics decision

Early instinct favored recall — surface every plausible supplier. In practice that was harmful, and the reason isn't ML. It's trust.

Recall-first instinct

Don't miss anyone who could supply it. More candidates, more quotes, more competition.

What false positives cost

Suppliers get low-quality RFQs and regret engaging. More suppliers quoting on the same RFQ reduces each supplier's chance of winning the PO, further depressing engagement. Ghosting rises on the next RFQ. Buyer trust degrades from noisy quotes. Ops cleanup grows.

Shifting to precision-first — actively suppressing low-confidence matches — improved supplier response rates ~21–25% over baseline in early signals and cut ops cleanup. An economic decision, not a metric optimization. It also surfaces a subtlety specific to supplier-level routing: the system matches on plausibility of supply, not confirmed possession. A supplier can be a reasonable match and still regret the RFQ for lacking that exact variant — invisible to quote-conversion, lethal to long-term trust.

The matching layer expresses this through confidence-floored buckets. Retrieved candidates are reduced to one best item per supplier, then sorted into tiers — and crucially, buckets are capacity, not quota. Empty slots are a feature. An RFQ with three genuine matches transmits to three suppliers, not ten padded ones.

05

Where rules helped, and where humans were mandatory

Matching was never a binary AI-vs-human switch. It was a layered decision system where each component does what it's actually good at.

Where deterministic rules earned their place

Rules and keyword matching gave a fast, debuggable, high-precision baseline for common SKUs, and stayed valuable as a diagnostic layer after embeddings arrived. In the final design — LLM making every match decision — deterministic checks guard the plumbing around the model: normalization symmetry, unit canonicality, routing-safety invariants (no supplier gets a line they didn't match; nothing below the floor auto-transmits; external never auto-transmits). They can't judge a match, but they catch the silent regressions that would poison the model's inputs.

Where humans were non-negotiable

Some decisions stayed human not because the model was immature, but because the cost of error is irreversible. Humans bracket the system on both ends — interpreting ambiguous inputs before they enter the pipeline, and reviewing risky matches before they reach a supplier.

  • Ambiguous RFQ specs, at the input — when a buyer's line item is unclear, underspecified, or uses non-standard shorthand, ops interprets and disambiguates it before it enters the normalizer. A clean, intentional spec in beats a confident misread downstream — and it keeps the structuring step honest rather than guessing.
  • Supplier legitimacy — a wrong call here isn't a bad match, it's a bad actor in the funnel.
  • First-time & high-risk RFQs — no track record to lean on, maximum downside.
  • Edge-case fabrication — where the right answer needs a conversation, not a classification.
  • Every low-confidence and alternate-brand match, at the output — confirmed before a supplier is ever bothered.

This input-side role stays consistent with a deliberately non-blocking design: ops disambiguates when a spec is genuinely unclear, but the system never stalls — unclear lines proceed at low confidence and are caught downstream, with most enrichment happening once suppliers engage.

The routing decision is gated by confidence: high-confidence brand-and-product matches transmit automatically; everything else routes to an ops reviewer. One detail makes supplier-level routing humane — a supplier only ever receives the specific lines they matched, with the matched item and per-attribute reasoning attached, never the full RFQ blindly. That's both the audit trail and the reason the supplier doesn't feel spammed.

Automation was earned, category by category — not assumed. The core judgment was deciding where not to automate yet.
06

The system, end to end

Every principle so far is one lane of a single pipeline — a sequence of stages where work shifts deliberately between rules, retrieval, the model, and humans, each placed where it's strong.

Two ideas govern the wiring. Catalog and RFQ pass through the same normalization before they meet — the symmetry that reconciles "Siemens" with "Siemens India." And the flow is recall-first then precision-gated: cast wide in retrieval because nothing downstream recovers a miss, then narrow hard through LLM judgment and confidence floors before anything reaches a supplier.

Read the colors and the design reads itself: rules guard the rails, retrieval casts wide, the model decides, humans hold the irreversible calls. No single component is trusted to do a job it's bad at — which is the whole point.
07

How I'd design the evals

Evals exist to make a non-deterministic system debuggable — and they're worthless until they're grounded in looking at real failures.

What existed

Honest current state: evaluation ran on manual spot checks, ops feedback loops, and business metrics as proxies. Ground truth existed but was deliberately narrow — ~50–55 catalogs validated for completeness, ~300 items mapped to known high-confidence suppliers. Diagnostic, not statistically representative; the job was to surface failure modes early and stop the team optimizing against misleading offline metrics. What follows is what I'd design next on that base.

How I'd evaluate — the method

A four-tier stack, where the expensive human work feeds the cheap automated checks rather than the reverse.

What I'd measure

Method sits above metrics; the metrics fall into three buckets, ordered the way the system behaves — inputs first, because they predict everything downstream.

1 · Input quality (leading indicators). Spec completeness (tolerances, ranges, materials, standards), process and certification coverage, catalog freshness and structural integrity. If these degrade, matching degrades regardless of model choice — and they move before outcomes do.

2 · Confidence-bucket performance. Quote and technical-approval rates for high-confidence matches; override rate and false-negative recovery for reviewed ones; and the suppressed-known-good rate against ground truth, so precision-tightening never silently drops capable suppliers. Above all, because nothing hard-filters, retrieval recall@K leads — a match absent from the candidate set is unrecoverable.

How precision is actually measured Precision-first only means something if precision is measured — and in a plausibility-of-supply system it has no single definition, so it's triangulated three ways, per bucket. Offline: true precision on the ~300-item ground truth (narrow but direct). Scaled: the validated LLM-judge plus ops override rate — a high override rate on a bucket is low precision there. Economic: supplier response and non-regret — precision as suppliers experience it. And because perfect precision is trivially gamed by routing almost nothing, the real target is precision at a fixed recall floor — clean matches subject to not dropping known-good suppliers. Suppressed-known-good and precision are the two ends of that one dial.

3 · Trust & outcome signals — read as two gates, not one:

North star

RFQ → valid-quote conversion, segmented by bucket. Did the match produce a quotable supplier?

Guardrail

Supplier response & non-regret rate (up ~21–25% over baseline in early signals). A system can maximize conversion while burning goodwill — and you won't see the damage until ghosting rises months later.

Optimize conversion; constrain on response. When they conflict — tightening floors lifts response but can drop coverage — response is the constraint you don't violate, because its damage is delayed and irreversible. A good match is defined by execution survival and preserved trust, not offline similarity. The loop is fed by structured ops feedback at the matching interface: a fixed-category verdict (brand-mismatch, taxonomy cross-mismatch, plausible-but-no-variant, retrieval-miss…) that does error analysis, grows the golden sets, validates the judge, and tunes the floors. One click, four jobs.

Drift is market movement, not model decay

In a marketplace, drift shows up less as model degradation than as the market moving — supplier capability changes, catalog obsolescence, buyer-intent shifts. So it's monitored as business risk, through leading indicators: rising override rates, supplier ghosting patterns, and conversion drops isolated to AI-suggested matches. Those signals — not a model-decay metric — tell you when to tighten thresholds or expand human review.

08

What this proves

This experience shaped how I approach AI products — less as a search for better models, more as a discipline of knowing where models belong.

  • Models are components, not solutions. The leverage was in data formation, normalization, and risk boundaries — not model choice.
  • Matching quality is capped by input clarity. Cleaner catalogs and structured RFQs beat any ranking tweak. Garbage in is unrecoverable downstream.
  • Precision-first preserves market trust. In a system where false positives cost relationships, suppressing weak matches is an economic strategy, not a metric.
  • Retrieve broad, reason narrow. Embeddings and keywords carry recall; soft biases shape ranking; the LLM decides — and auditability comes from its per-attribute reasoning.
  • Automation must be earned. Confidence gates, not assumptions, decide when the system acts. Some decisions stay human permanently.
  • Evals track execution survival, not offline accuracy. A good match is defined by quote conversion and preserved trust — grounded in humans looking at real failures.
The hardest decisions in this system were never modeling decisions. They were judgments about where to be broad, where to be strict, and where to refuse to automate — and how to know, from real supplier behavior, whether those judgments were right.
