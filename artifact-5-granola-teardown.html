The Discipline of Subtraction What Granola removed, and why it mattered more than what it added.

the bot + the stored audio = the product
A teardown of Granola
On the difference between what an architecture forces and what a team chooses.

On a customer call last month, a procurement executive at an industrial buyer disclosed something about how his industry actually makes vendor decisions — an honest, slightly uncomfortable truth he would not have said in the first thirty seconds if a bot had announced it was recording. I noticed it because I noticed I hadn't noticed the recording.

Over the following weeks, my own behavior shifted. I started using Granola for offline 1:1s and ad-hoc discussions, not just calls — it became a queryable record of decisions and reasoning that saved me from re-explaining context. More interesting: my team was visibly more honest about deadlines, risks, and tradeoffs in meetings they perceived as unrecorded, even though Granola was generating notes the entire time. Surveillance perception drives behavior more than surveillance fact.

Granola's defining decision isn't what it added. It's what it removed.

01 — THE FRAME EVERYONE ELSE USES

The popular reading

The dominant reading of Granola runs through Karpathy's autonomy slider — Granola sits at a deliberate middle point, the user types what matters, the AI extends rather than replaces. That reading describes what the product feels like. It doesn't explain what made the architecture possible, why competitors imitating the feel can't replicate the economics, or what locks Granola out of the verticals it can't enter. The partial-autonomy UI is a design choice layered on top of a more interesting structural decision. This teardown is about the structural decision.

02 — THE LOAD-BEARING DECISIONS

Two subtractions, not one

Every meeting notes tool makes two independent structural decisions: how the audio enters the system, and whether to keep it after. Granola made the unusual choice on both.

Subtraction one — no bot. Granola captures system audio at the OS level instead of joining the call as a visible participant. Otter, Fireflies, Fathom, and Read AI all send a bot. Granola doesn't.

Subtraction two — no stored audio. The audio is transcribed in real time and discarded. The transcript persists; the source does not. Most competitors retain audio and often video on their servers.

The two decisions are independent — a tool could keep one and drop the other. Nobody does. Granola made both restraint choices at once, and each produces its own downstream cascade.

03 — THE CASCADE

What the subtractions produce

Trust preservation. No bot means no announcement, no disclosure dynamic. The conversation stays in its natural state. This opens a TAM bot-based tools struggle to enter — investor pitches, hiring conversations, sensitive customer research, sales calls where candor sets pricing, internal meetings where teams calibrate against perceived surveillance. The cold-start cohort of VCs and founders wasn't a marketing accident. It was the direct consequence of being usable in the meetings other tools made worse.

Platform and context agnosticism. Bot-based architecture can't capture an in-person 1:1, a phone call, a hallway conversation, or a voice note dictated while driving. Granola can — audio is captured at the OS level regardless of source. Picture being three minutes late for a Zoom call. You don't check Zoom version support, don't paste a link, don't wait for a bot to load. You hit record and join. Bot-based competitors have caught up on platform breadth. What they can't match is the cognitive certainty that the product works regardless of what's producing the audio.

Smaller compliance surface. No persistent audio means no audio liability — no perpetual storage exposure, no discovery target in litigation, no question of where audio of meeting X is stored when enterprise security reviews ask. The highest-risk artifact never exists. Granola can tell enterprise buyers "we don't have your audio" and mean it — a cleaner privacy story than competitors who must explain how they keep audio secure. The transcript is its own liability, but a smaller and more contained one than audio plus metadata. The same property is a structural enterprise advantage where audio storage is the risk, and a disqualifier where audio is the regulatory record. Same architecture, opposite verdicts.

Three structural consequences from two restraint decisions. Competitors who kept the bot and kept the audio cannot reach this position without redesigning their architecture from underneath the product.

04 — THE CEILING

Where the philosophy breaks

The moat

Subtraction is what makes the trust, the reach, and the clean compliance story possible.

The ceiling

The same subtraction is what locks Granola out of the verticals where audio is the record.

Every load-bearing design decision creates the moat and the ceiling at the same time.

Compliance-mandated industries

SEBI requires Indian stockbrokers to record dealer conversations on turrets and mobile phones for regulatory inspection. RBI imposes parallel rules on banks for customer dispute trails. MiFID II in Europe and FINRA in the US do the same. These regimes don't ask for a record of what was discussed — they require the original communication preserved in non-rewritable form. A transcript is a derivative artifact; audio is the source. Granola has the transcript but not the source. The subtraction that won prosumer trust is the same subtraction that locks Granola out of the highest-ACV verticals.

Unrecoverable transcription error

Without audio playback, a transcription mistake has no source of truth to return to. Speaker identification is also structurally weaker — bot-based tools join the call as a participant and can map audio to identity directly; Granola captures system audio and has to guess. Granola supports ten languages while Fireflies supports sixty-plus — a structural consequence, not a coincidence. Accent-heavy contexts, multilingual organizations, and jargon-dense industries sit outside the addressable market because the architecture can't audit its own errors.

Governed enterprise expansion

The "your data on your machine" wedge becomes "data is locked in" friction. Disclosure features are off by default. Integrations are deliberately limited. The locked-cache incident — partner workflows breaking when Granola tightened local data access — was the first public symptom. IT-administered governance is fundamentally incompatible with user-administered subtraction. The 2026 API launch is the partial walk-back.

Awareness erosion

The trust advantage depends on participants being unaware of background capture. As Granola scales, sophisticated participants will start asking — and the conversation reverts to the same disclosure dynamic Granola was built to avoid. The architectural advantage is durable; the social advantage has a half-life that shortens with every new user.

05 — THE LESSON, APPLIED

The harder question

The interesting question for anyone building AI inside an existing product isn't what to add. It's what to subtract. Three examples worth thinking through.

Microsoft Copilot in Word subtract a surface

Word now opens new documents and new sections with a "Draft with Copilot" affordance — a proactive offer to start the writing for you before you've typed a word. The subtraction case is structural: remove the affordance. Copilot stays available behind a keyboard shortcut, invoked only when the writer decides they need it. Writing is thinking, and an interface that offers to think for you before you've started trains you out of the habit.

The trade: Microsoft won't make this move because visible AI justifies the $30/user upsell — the subtraction is right for users and wrong for revenue, which is the trade most enterprise AI products are silently choosing against the user.
Notion AI subtract a distribution

AI in Notion is spatially everywhere — slash-AI in every block, sparkle icons on every property, Ask AI buttons on every doc, AI in search, AI in database views. The argument that each surface serves a distinct intent stops holding past four or five surfaces. Past that, AI everywhere becomes AI nowhere — users pattern-match the icons as visual noise. The subtraction case isn't a single AI surface; it's the smallest number of distinct invocation points that covers genuinely different intents. The cost of spatial sprawl is cognitive, not technical.

Cursor & GitHub Copilot subtract what can't be audited

I remember when the debate was whether AI should suggest as you type or wait to be invoked — type-and-suggest versus chat-and-edit. That debate is over. Cursor's Composer, GitHub Copilot Edits, and Windsurf's Cascade have all moved to multi-file agentic editing. But the subtraction discipline relocates rather than ends. Research found developers accept inline suggestions at rates that track the AI's confidence, not its correctness (Perry et al., Stanford 2022). The same pattern now operates at the agent layer: humans approve pull requests of thousands of lines they could not have written and cannot fully review.

The new case: bound agent output to what a human can meaningfully review in one pass. The layer has moved from how AI generates to whether the human review step is real or theatrical.
Copilotsubtracts a surface.
Notionsubtracts a spatial distribution.
Cursorsubtracts what the human can no longer audit.
Granolasubtracted a participant and a storage policy.

The discipline is the same; the form changes at every layer.

What is your product adding because the AI category demands it, that it should be subtracting instead?
